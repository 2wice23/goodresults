What Is a Comp?
Comparable sales, ARV, and why they matter
3 min ▼

A comparable sale (comp) is a recently sold property in the same neighborhood with similar characteristics. We use comps to establish the ARV (After Repair Value) — what the property will sell for after full renovation.

ARV is your foundation. Everything else — repair estimates, MAO calculations, offer pricing — flows from ARV. If your comp analysis is wrong, your entire deal math is wrong.

The Golden Rule

A soft comp takes 3 minutes. Not 10. If you're spending more than 5 minutes on a soft comp, you're overthinking it. Quick wins beat perfect analysis.

You're not a professional appraiser. Your job is to qualify deals quickly — identify whether a property has potential before spending time on detailed due diligence.

Comp Rules & Selection
Time window, location, size, and red flags
4 min ▼

Not all sales are good comps. Here are the rules that separate clean data from noise:

CriterionRule
Time Window12 months max — Gayden explicitly: "Our comps need to be within 12 months."
LocationSame neighborhood, same side of major roads/barriers. Do not comp across highways.
Square FootageSimilar size. Large differences (600+ sqft) get separate comps, not price/sqft adjustments.
ADU / Extra UnitsNot clean comps. Deduct $30-40k or discard entirely.
DOM (Days on Market)High DOM signals price or condition problems — red flag.
Key Point

Model-match means similar in age, style, condition, and size — not necessarily exact square footage. Look for like-for-like trades, not perfectly identical properties.

Running Comps by Market
Nevada (Zillow) vs Texas (PropStream)
3 min ▼

Your tools differ by market. Nevada agents learn from YouTube (Jerry Norton, Jamil Damji). Texas has more detailed data available. Here's the exact workflow:

Nevada — Use Zillow (3-minute soft comp):

  1. Pull up the subject property on Zillow
  2. Open the map view
  3. Draw a tight boundary around the immediate neighborhood (1-2 residential streets)
  4. Filter: Sold, last 12 months, similar square footage
  5. Identify 2-3 model-match or near-match sales

Texas — Use PropStream if available (3-minute soft comp):

  1. Pull the subject property into PropStream
  2. Filter by 12-month sold comps, same neighborhood
  3. Pull 2-3 best matches
  4. If PropStream isn't available, fall back to Zillow using the Nevada method
Watch & Learn

Agents learn comps from YouTube videos by Jerry Norton and Jamil Damji. This is your primary learning source. Watch how they think about comparables.

MAO Formulas by Market
Texas (75% minus repairs) vs Nevada (69% flat)
4 min ▼

MAO (Maximum Allowable Offer) is the most you should offer to still make money. It's based on the 70% rule but customized by market:

MarketFormulaNotes
TexasMAO = (ARV × 0.75) − Repair EstimateCheaper real estate, higher margin potential. Repair costs matter more.
NevadaMAO = ARV × 0.69More expensive real estate. Flat 69% — no separate repair deduction in most cases.

The 30% buffer covers: carrying costs (loan interest, property taxes during hold), closing costs, commissions, and your profit margin.

Example — Texas Deal: ARV $280K, 1,800 sqft at $35/sqft ($63K repairs) → MAO = (280,000 × 0.75) − 63,000 = 210,000 − 63,000 = $147,000

Example — Nevada Deal: ARV $280K → MAO = 280,000 × 0.69 = $193,200

Critical Rule

MAO is internal ONLY. Never share the number with realtors or sellers. Share comps, repair estimate, reasoning. The offer price is what matters — not how you got there.

Repair Cost Framework
$20, $35, or $60 per square foot — judgment call
3 min ▼

You are not a contractor. But you need to estimate repair scope quickly. Here's the framework:

Scope$/sqftWhat's Included
Cosmetic Only$20/sqftPaint, flooring, minor fixtures
Standard Full Reno$35/sqftKitchen, bathrooms, floors, paint throughout
Heavy Reno$60/sqftRoof, HVAC, electrical, plumbing, structural

These are your starting points. As you see more properties, you'll refine your estimates. A property with a 1992 HVAC and a 20-year-old roof pushes toward $60/sqft. A cosmetic-only flip stays at $20/sqft.

When to Escalate

When a property has a clearly unique or complex repair profile, call Gayden before making an offer. These estimates are judgment calls — they improve with reps.

Never Walk a Property Before Contract
The objection script that works
3 min ▼

Acquisition agents do NOT walk properties before contract. If a realtor insists, use this script. It's been tested and works:

Walkthrough Objection Response (Full Version)

"I'm not a contractor. I barely own a hammer. Me walking through the property doesn't tell me anything useful. My contractors charge $600–800 a day. In order to send my contractor, I need to be in contract first — otherwise I'm spending a thousand bucks on inspections to find out we can't even agree on a price. That doesn't make sense at the volume I operate. Give me a 5-day DD and my contractor is there the next morning."

Shorter version: "I pay someone to hang pictures at my house. My contractors charge $600–800/day. I need to be in contract first. 5-day DD and my guys are there next day."

Choose the version based on the realtor's personality and conversation flow. If they push back a second time, escalate to Gayden. Don't fight about it yourself.

Explaining Your Price to Realtors
Show comps and reasoning — never reveal MAO
3 min ▼

When a realtor asks "How did you get to this price?", they deserve an answer — just not the full MAO formula. Here's what you say:

Realtor Conversation

Them: "That's too low. How did you get to $147,000?"

You: "Here's what I'm seeing in comps — three recent sales in your neighborhood at $195, $198, and $201 per square foot. This property needs $35 per square foot in standard renovation. After accounting for carrying costs, closing, and margin, that's where I land. Happy to walk through the comps if you want to see them."

You're explaining reasoning, not formulas. Comps → repair estimate → market conditions = your offer. Never say "my MAO is X" or "I use the 70% rule."

Realtor-Facing vs. Internal Math

The MAO formulas are internal numbers only. What you tell a realtor is the offer price — you never share the formula, the MAO, or how you arrived at the number. Explain reasoning (ARV, repair estimate, market conditions) if pushed.
