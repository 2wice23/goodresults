01 — What is a Comp?

What is a Comp?

A comparable sale (comp) is a recently sold property in the same neighborhood with similar characteristics. We use comps to establish the ARV (After Repair Value) — what the property will sell for after full renovation.

ARV is the foundation of every offer. The higher the ARV, the higher the MAO (Maximum Allowable Offer). The lower the ARV, the lower the offer. Comps determine pricing. Learning to run them quickly and accurately is a core skill.

02 — The Rules of Comps

The Rules of Comps

Every comp you pull must follow these rules. Break them and your analysis is useless.

RuleStandardWhy It Matters
Time Window12 months maximumGayden explicitly: "Our comps need to be within 12 months." Older data = stale market.
LocationSame neighborhood, same side of major roadsDo not comp across highways or major barriers. Different sides = different buyer pools.
Square FootageSimilar size, no price/sqft math for big diffsA 600 sqft difference gets its own comp, not a multiplier adjustment.
ADU / Extra UnitsDiscard or deduct $30-40kA comp with an ADU is not clean. Either throw it out or subtract $30-40k from value.
Days on Market (DOM)Pay attention to high DOMHigh DOM signals either price problem or condition problem. It's a red flag on your comp list.
Critical Rule

When in doubt about a comp, throw it out. A bad comp cascades bad pricing into your offer. Better to walk on a deal than offer on bad math.

03 — How to Run Comps (By Market)

How to Run Comps (By Market)

A soft comp takes 3 minutes. Not 10. Not 15. If you're spending more than 5 minutes, you're overthinking it. The goal is speed with accuracy.

+
Nevada — Zillow 3-Minute Method Step-by-step
▼

Step 1: Pull up the subject property on Zillow.

Step 2: Open the map view.

Step 3: Draw a boundary around the immediate neighborhood using Zillow's draw tool. This is usually 1-2 residential streets max — not the whole city.

Step 4: Filter: Sold, last 12 months, similar square footage (within 200-300 sqft).

Step 5: Identify 2–3 model-match or near-match sales. These are your comps.

Step 6: If the first comp you find has an ADU → discard it or adjust down $30-40k.

You now have your ARV. If the sold price range is $250-270k, your ARV estimate is roughly $260k. Done.

+
Texas — PropStream Preferred, Zillow Fallback Same 3-minute rule applies
▼

If PropStream is available, use it — it's more detailed and faster for Texas deals. If not available, fall back to Zillow using the same Nevada methodology above.

The principle is identical: 12 months, same neighborhood, similar sqft, 2-3 comps, done in 3 minutes.

Soft Comp Rule

Soft comp = 3 minutes max. This is a quick market check to know if a deal is worth pursuing. For full, detailed comps, that's Joe's domain — you run quick checks to qualify deals.

04 — ARV Calculation

What is ARV and How It's Used

ARV (After Repair Value) is the estimated market value of the property after full renovation. It's based on comps — what similar fully-renovated homes in the same area are selling for.

ARV is not the purchase price. It's not a guess. It's a data-driven estimate built on comparable sales in the last 12 months.

Once you have ARV, everything flows from there: repair estimate, MAO formula, and final offer price.

05 — MAO Formula By Market

The Maximum Allowable Offer (MAO)

MAO is the highest price we can offer and still hit our profit target. It's an internal number — never share it with realtors or sellers.

+
Texas Formula: (ARV × 0.75) − Repair Estimate Example worked through
▼

Formula: MAO = (ARV × 0.75) − Repair Estimate

Example Property:

ARV: $280,000 (based on comps)

Square Footage: 1,800 sqft

Condition: Dated, cosmetic needed, kitchen/bath reno required

Repair Estimate: $35/sqft (standard full reno) × 1,800 = $63,000

Calculation:

($280,000 × 0.75) − $63,000 = $210,000 − $63,000 = $147,000 MAO

Your offer: $147,000 (or lower if you want higher margin)

+
Nevada Formula: ARV × 0.69 (flat) Example worked through
▼

Formula: MAO = ARV × 0.69

Nevada uses a flat multiplier. No separate repair deduction in most cases.

Example Property:

ARV: $280,000 (based on comps)

Calculation:

$280,000 × 0.69 = $193,200 MAO

Your offer: $193,200 (or lower if you want higher margin)

Exception: If property has major structural/system issues (foundation, roof collapse, electrical), discuss with Gayden before offering. You may go lower.

06 — The 70% Rule (Background)

Understanding the 70% Rule

The "70% rule" is a common investor shorthand: offer no more than 70% of ARV minus repairs. Good Results uses market-specific versions:

  • Texas = 75% minus repairs (cheaper real estate, higher margin potential)
  • Nevada = 69% flat (more expensive real estate, less repair deduction needed)

Why? The 30% (or 31%) buffer covers carrying costs, closing costs, commissions, and profit margin. This is the math that makes the deal feasible.

07 — Realtor-Facing vs Internal Math

Never Share Your MAO — Share Your Reasoning

MAO is internal only. The realtor and seller never hear the word "MAO" from you. Ever.

What you tell the realtor is the offer price. If they ask how you got to it, explain your reasoning: ARV (share your comps), repair estimate, market conditions. Never say "my MAO is X."

Realtor Response to "How Did You Get This Number?"
I'm seeing comps in this neighborhood close between $260-280k in the last 12 months. That's my ARV baseline. The property needs a kitchen/bath reno, new flooring, paint throughout — I'm budgeting about $35 per square foot for that. When I factor in my closing costs and market conditions, I land at $147. That's a fair offer for both of us. What does your seller think?
Critical Rule

MAO is internal ONLY. Never share the number. Share comps, repair estimate, reasoning. The offer price is what matters to the seller.

08 — Repair Cost Framework

Estimating Repair Costs ($20–$60/sqft)

You are not a contractor. But you need to estimate repair scope quickly. Here's the framework:

Scope$/sqftWhat's Included
Cosmetic Only$20/sqftPaint, flooring, minor fixtures
Standard Full Reno$35/sqftKitchen, bathrooms, floors, paint throughout
Heavy Reno$60/sqftRoof, HVAC, electrical, plumbing, structural

The last column is your starting point. As you see more properties, you'll refine these estimates. A property with a 1992 HVAC and a roof that's seen 20 years pushes toward the $60 range. A cosmetic-only property stays at $20.

Acquisition Agent's Discretion

When a property has a clearly unique or complex repair profile, call Gayden before making an offer. These estimates are judgment calls, and they improve with time and reps.

09 — Never Walk a Property Before Contract

The Walkthrough Objection Script

If a realtor insists you walk the property before contract, use this script. It's been tested and works.

Walkthrough Objection Response
I'm not a contractor. I pay someone to hang pictures at my house. My contractors charge $600–800 a day. I can't send them out until I have a contract — otherwise I'm spending a thousand bucks on inspections to find out we can't even agree on a price. That doesn't make sense at the volume I operate. Give me a 5-day DD and my contractor is there next day. We run all the inspections during that window, and if something crazy comes up, we deal with it then.

If a realtor pushes back a second time, escalate to Gayden. Don't fight about it yourself.
