Why Contracts Look Different By State
RPA in Nevada, TREC in Texas — and why we use state standard forms
2 min ▼

Nevada uses the RPA (Residential Purchase Agreement). Texas uses the TREC (Texas Real Estate Commission) standard form. Both are standard contracts in their respective states. Good Results uses these because they're what the listing agents know and expect.

We don't reinvent the wheel with custom attorney forms. We start with the state standard and layer in our four non-negotiable terms. This approach is faster, cleaner, and meets seller expectations.

Key Principle

Always use state standard forms. List agents are familiar with them. Sellers are comfortable with them. Adding our non-negotiable clauses is enough.

The Texas Option Fee
Why $1 is legal, valid, and intentional
2 min ▼

The option fee gives the buyer an unrestricted right to terminate during the option period. Good Results pays $1.00.

  • Market standard is $100–500. Our $1.00 is intentional.
  • It is a valid, legal amount. Sellers agree to it.
  • If a realtor pushes back: "If you want proof of seriousness, call Aaron at Spartan Title. He'll tell you how many deals we close."
  • If a seller absolutely insists on more: escalate to Gayden.
Not the Same as EMD

The option fee is NOT earnest money. The EMD (also due at end of option period) is separate. Both are nonrefundable after the option period expires.

Four Non-Negotiable Clauses
These appear on every Good Results offer, in both states
3 min ▼

These four clauses appear on every Good Results offer in Nevada and Texas. They're non-negotiable — we don't remove them:

ClauseWhat It Says
Investor Intent ClauseBuyer is a real estate investor, does not intend to occupy as primary residence, plans to profit through renovation and/or resale.
Assignment ClauseBuyer reserves the right to assign this contract. (We may close in a different entity or partner with a contractor/co-investor.)
Cash OfferNo financing contingency, no appraisal contingency.
As-IsNo repair requests from seller. Buyer accepts property in current condition.
Why Be Transparent?

Contractor inspections reveal rehab plans anyway. Better to disclose upfront that you're an investor than for the seller to find out mid-deal. Honesty builds trust and prevents last-minute surprises.

Assignment Clause — If It's Rejected
Escalation path and the double-close option
2 min ▼

Some sellers object to the assignment clause. Here's the process:

  1. First: Use objection handlers to KEEP assignment language. Explain we're investors, operate 6+ LLCs, and need entity flexibility for capital purposes.
  2. If seller keeps fighting it: Talk to Gayden.
  3. Offer removal: You can still double-close if needed. Costs more ($1–2K) but doesn't require seller permission.
  4. Last-minute play: Gayden has gotten sellers to sign assignment agreements the day before closing by explaining it's needed for entity purposes. Works when you have a strong relationship.
Why We Keep It

Assignment allows us to close through whichever entity has available capital. It's not about hiding anything — it's about operational flexibility.

EMD & Option Fee — Due at End of Period
Not 3 calendar days. Not right away. At the deadline.
2 min ▼

Both the option fee AND earnest money are due at the end of the option/DD period (days 7–10 for Texas, day 7 for Nevada) — NOT 3 calendar days from acceptance. This is a common mistake.

Verify exact timing on the contract. Missing this deadline gives the seller the right to terminate. Do not be late. Mark it on your calendar the moment the contract is signed.

Timeline Clarity

Day 1 = first day after acceptance. EMD + Option Fee both due on Day 7 (Nevada) or by Day 7–10 (Texas). Check contract language for exact time (often 5pm local time).

The Double Close Option
When assignment isn't possible, close twice instead
2 min ▼

If a seller absolutely refuses assignment, you can still get the deal done with a double close. You close on the property from the original seller, then immediately resell it to your end buyer. Both closings can happen on the same day.

Cost: Typically $1,000–2,000 in title and coordination fees. Requires coordination with title company. No seller permission needed — it's your deal once you own it.

In practice: Assignment is preferred (faster, cheaper), but double-close is the backup when a seller won't budge on the assignment clause. It works, and deals still close profitably.

Approval Required

Double-close decisions should be discussed with Gayden or Joe before committing. Title and financing logistics need lead time.
