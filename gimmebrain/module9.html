01 — Why Contracts Look Different By State

Why Contracts Look Different By State

Nevada uses the RPA (Residential Purchase Agreement). Texas uses the TREC (Texas Real Estate Commission) standard form. Both are the standard contracts that listing agents in each state know and expect.

Good Results uses these standard forms because they're what the other side expects. Showing up with a custom contract you had an attorney draw slows deals and raises red flags. We use the standard form and add our required terms within it.

02 — The DD / Option Period Comparison

The DD / Option Period Comparison

The key difference between states is the inspection/due diligence period. Here's the full breakdown:

Key point: In Texas, BOTH the option fee AND earnest money are due at the END of the DD/option period (typically days 7–10), NOT within 3 calendar days of acceptance.

03 — The Texas Option Fee Explained

The Texas Option Fee Explained

The option fee gives the buyer an unrestricted right to terminate the contract during the option period without penalty (except losing the option fee).

Good Results pays $1.00.

Market standard is $100–$500. Our $1.00 is intentional and legal. Sellers agree to it because it's so low and because the industry standard is so variable.

Option Fee Key Points

The option fee is NOT earnest money. They are separate.
Both are due at end of the option period (not 3 calendar days from acceptance).

04 — Non-Negotiable Terms on Every Offer

Non-Negotiable Terms on Every Offer

These 4 clauses appear on every single Good Results offer, regardless of state:

1. Investor Intent Clause

Buyer is a real estate investor. Does not intend to occupy as primary residence. Plans to make profit through renovation and/or resale.

2. Assignment Clause

Buyer reserves the right to assign this contract. (Reason: we may close in a different entity or partner with contractors/co-investors.)

3. Cash Offer

No financing contingency. No appraisal contingency. Cash buyer, 10–21 day close.

4. As-Is

Property purchased as-is. No repair requests from seller. Buyer responsible for all repairs/updates post-closing.

Investor Intent Explanation Script
"We're real estate investors. We acquire properties, fix them up, and resell them for profit. That's why the investor intent clause is in the contract — it's standard for us."
05 — Assignment vs Removing It

Assignment vs Removing It

If a seller objects to the assignment clause:

Option 1: Offer to remove it. Then you're obligated to take title yourself, but you can still double-close if needed (you close on the property, then immediately resell it).

Option 2: Keep it. Explain why: "We operate through multiple entities for tax and operational reasons. The assignment clause gives us flexibility to close through whichever entity has available capital at closing."

Gayden's 25th-Hour Play

When a deal is falling apart and you're hours from closing, Gayden has gotten sellers to sign assignment agreements the day before closing by explaining: "I need this for entity purposes, and we're already closing tomorrow anyway." It works when you have a strong relationship and the seller is already motivated.

Why Keep Assignment In?

We operate 6+ LLCs. Capital sits in different entities. Some deals we partner with contractors who bring capital. Assignment lets us close through the entity that's ready, not force restructuring.

What is a double close exactly?
⌄

A double close is two closings that happen on the same day (or within hours): (1) You close on the property from the seller, and (2) You immediately close on the property to your end buyer.

Why? If assignment is removed from the contract, you have to take title yourself. A double close lets you take title and resell within minutes without waiting.

How it works: Your title company coordinates both. You wire funds for side A (original purchase), take title for seconds, then side B funds come in (your buyer wires), you close to them, you keep the spread, seller gets paid, and your buyer gets title. All on the same day.

Cost: $1,000–$2,000 in additional title coordination fees. Only worth it if your margin justifies it.

+
What happens if we miss the 3-day option fee deadline?
⌄

If you miss the 3-calendar-day deadline for the option fee (in Texas), the seller has the right to terminate the contract. You lose the deal.

This is why it matters. It's not just a technicality — missing the deadline gives the seller an out if they change their mind or get a better offer. Don't be late on this deadline.

Good process: Wire option fee within 2 days to be safe. Don't wait until day 3.

+
Why do we keep the investor intent clause in?
⌄

The investor intent clause tells the seller upfront: "We're not owner-occupants. We're buying to fix and flip (or hold and rent)."

Why not hide it? Because it comes out in inspection. A contractor comes to inspect the property, takes photos, makes notes about rehab, and the seller figures it out anyway. Better to be transparent at the start than have it be a surprise mid-deal.

Does it hurt? Not really. Most distressed sellers prefer cash buyers who will buy as-is. They don't care if you're an investor — they care that you close fast and take the property off their hands.
