Section 1

Always Lead With Cash

When any realtor, seller, or agent asks how you fund deals, your answer is simple and consistent: "We're a cash buyer. We close in 10–21 days, no bank approval needed, no appraisal contingency." Don't elaborate unless directly pressed. Good Results buys with TRUE CASH from our bank account. Period. No hard money. No private money. No borrowing from anyone. No lender permission needed.

This isn't deception. It's positioning. The mechanisms underneath don't matter to the realtor. What matters is whether the deal closes, on time, without contingencies. That's what "cash" signals.

Section 2

The Funding Stack (Internal Reality)

Behind the "cash" label, there are multiple mechanisms. You need to understand all of them — both for structuring deals and for the rare moments when realtors or sellers press for details.

Source What Realtors Hear Internal Reality
Cash Fast, 10–21 day close, no bank, no appraisal May be hard money or private money underneath
Hard Money Internal mechanism only Short-term asset-based loan
Owner Finance Creative option for motivated sellers Seller carries the note
Private Money Internal only Investor capital
Script — When Pressed Harder
"We use a combination of capital sources — it all closes like cash to you. No bank contingency, no appraisal, you just need to make sure title is clear."
Section 3

Hard Money, Owner Finance & Private Money

Hard Money: Short-term, asset-backed loans from private lenders. Good Results uses when needed. DO NOT lead with this — it raises questions about stability. The realtor doesn't need to know it's hard money. They need to know it closes like cash.

Owner Financing: Seller acts as the bank — buyer makes monthly payments directly to seller. Used primarily with highly motivated/distressed sellers who need monthly income or have specific tax situations. Good Results uses this for certain manufactured home portfolio deals where the buyer can't qualify for specialty lenders.

Private Money: Capital from private investors and partners. This stays internal. Never discussed with realtors or public-facing conversations. It's one part of your funding stack.

Golden Rule

The mechanism doesn't matter to the realtor. What matters is certainty. Will the deal close on time, without contingencies? If yes, you're a cash buyer in their eyes.

Section 4

Manufactured Homes: Special Financing Warning

Multi-moved manufactured homes (moved 2+ times) have a very limited buyer pool because standard financing (FHA/conventional) is not available. Only specialty lenders (21st Mortgage Corporation, Credit Human, Triad) will touch them, and their requirements are strict:

1

Minimum 660 credit score — no exceptions for lower credit

2

5% of borrower's own funds as down payment

3

~8% interest rate — higher than conventional

4

25-year term — standard for specialty lenders

The buyer pool that qualifies: someone with $50K–$80K in savings, 660+ credit, stable income. That's not the pool buying mobile homes. This creates a very small buyer pool.

Section 5

Why This Matters for Realtors

Realtors have been burned. They've had a "cash buyer" come in, get a deal under contract, and then reveal financing contingencies mid-closing. The deal falls apart. The realtor looks bad. Their client is angry. Realtors remember this.

When you say cash, you're making a promise. Your entire credibility rests on follow-through. Every deal that closes on time, without contingency surprises, creates a realtor who will send you more deals. Your reputation is your most valuable asset in this business.

Script — If There's Doubt
"If you're concerned, call Aaron at my title company — he'll tell you exactly what our track record looks like. We close deals on time, period."

What exactly is hard money and why do we use it?
The mechanism behind the scenes
▼

Hard money is a short-term loan from a private lender secured by the property itself. Instead of a bank evaluating your credit score and income history, the hard lender evaluates the property's value and your exit strategy.

Why we use it: When we need to close fast without waiting for traditional bank approval (which takes 30–45 days), hard money lets us fund the deal in days. It costs more in interest (typically 10–15% annually), but it closes like cash to the seller. We never volunteer this info to the realtor.

The realtor doesn't care if it's hard money underneath. They care that the deal closes. And hard money deals close.

+
What makes owner financing work for motivated sellers?
Why some sellers prefer monthly payments

Some sellers owe little or nothing on the property and would rather receive monthly payments (steady income, tax spread across years) than a lump sum. This is especially relevant for manufactured home deals and sellers with IRS complications.

Example: Seller owns a mobile home free and clear. Selling for $80K as a lump sum = $80K taxable income in one year. Owner financing the same property for $100K over 10 years = $10K income per year = spread tax liability. For sellers with complicated tax situations, this is attractive.

Gayden negotiates these directly when the seller motivation and property situation align. It's a niche strategy, not a primary play, but it's powerful when it works.
