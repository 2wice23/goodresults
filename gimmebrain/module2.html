Realtor Outreach & Calls

Master the phone skills, scripts, and relationship-building strategies to source deals directly from realtors and build a sustainable acquisition machine.

01 — The Goal of a Cold Call

The Goal of a Cold Call

Get them to trust you enough to send you a deal. That's it. Don't pitch too hard. Don't explain everything. Make them feel heard.

02 — Call Volume is Everything

Call Volume is Everything

100 calls/day is the target. Training phase: 50+ calls/day is acceptable. This is a phone-call-heavy business. Texts open pathways to calls, but calls get deals.

03 — Opening Every Cold Call

Opening Every Cold Call

The opening is three steps. Always the same structure. Never skip steps. The opening is where you establish credibility and permission to pitch.

Step 1: State your name and company. Make it fast, no fluff. "Hey, it's [Your Name] with Good Results." Step 2: Qualify them with a yes/no question — do they work with cash buyers or have off-market/investor-friendly properties? This tells you immediately if they're worth talking to. Step 3: Based on their answer, deliver the buy box or permission-to-follow-up close and move on.

Your opening is their first impression of you. It should be professional but casual. Natural but directed. You're not reading a script robotically — you're having a conversation with someone you actually want to talk to. Realtors can hear when you're reading versus when you're connecting. Make it real.

Sample Opener (Copyable)
"Hey, is this [Name]? Hey, what's up — it's [Your Name] with Good Results. Quick question — do you ever get off-market properties or investor-friendly stuff? ... Yeah? Awesome. Let me tell you a little about what I'm looking for."

Notice what this opener does: It confirms you have the right person (establishes connection). It identifies who you are and the company (credibility). It asks a binary question that requires a yes/no or maybe (qualification). If yes, you move to your 30-second buy box. If no, you ask permission to follow up and hang up — don't waste time on someone who doesn't work with investors. If maybe, you dig in: "When you do get something investor-friendly, would you shoot me a quick text?"

The goal is to give them a way to say yes, a way to say no, and move accordingly. No objection handling yet. That comes later. Right now, you're just qualifying if they're even worth the next conversation.

04 — The Buy Box Pitch (30 seconds)

The Buy Box Pitch (30 seconds)

Once they've said yes, you deliver the buy box. This should take about 30 seconds. It covers what you buy, how you buy, why they should care, and what you need from them. Nothing more. No fluff. No explanation of your entire operation.

The pitch has five key elements: (1) Cash buyer — lead with this every time, it's your superpower. (2) Close speed — "10 to 21 days, as fast as possible" — NOT "20-30 days." (3) As-is purchase — any condition, no repair requests, no contingencies. (4) No contingencies — no appraisal, no inspection contingency, no bank involvement, no nonsense. (5) Easy relationship — you're not going to tour the house five times, you're not going to nickel-and-dime them at closing.

Buy Box Pitch — 30 Seconds (Copyable)
"We're cash buyers. We close as fast as possible — usually 10 to 21 days — no bank, no appraisal, no conditions. We buy as-is — any condition. We're in [market]. One contract, one check."

Critical detail: Never say "20–30 days." The message is "fast as possible — 10 to 21 days." The range matters. 20–30 days sounds like a bank timeline, which defeats the purpose of being a cash buyer. 10–21 sounds like you can move in as few as 10 if they're motivated, but you're realistically closing in 2–3 weeks. That's the psychological message. It feels faster while being honest about typical timelines.

Also notice: You don't proactively mention commission. If a realtor asks "How much are you offering the agent?", you confirm it — you work with a 2.5–3% agent commission standard (full amount, no negotiation down). But you don't lead with it. You're not trying to negotiate their commission down or act cheap. You're leading with speed and ease as your differentiators. That's what solves their problem.

Never Say These Words in FIRST Cold Outreach SMS

Never use "wholesale," "wholesaler," "assign," or describe yourself as an "investor" in your FIRST TEXT MESSAGE to cold realtors you don't yet know. These terms can trigger past bad experiences with other investors, so keep initial outreach professional: "We purchase properties as-is for cash. Fast close, no contingencies." However, "flip" and "fix-and-flip" ARE acceptable on calls, in follow-up SMS, and follow-up conversations — just not in that first cold text. In calls, you can freely say "cash buyer," "fix and flip," and reference that you invest in properties. The key is tone: on calls, your voice protects you. In cold SMS, your only tool is professionalism.

+
Why never mention commission proactively? 3 min read
▼

Realtors are incredibly sensitive about commission. They've been burned by investors who promise 3% and then negotiate down to 1% after the contract is signed, forcing them to choose between a lower commission or no deal. You don't want to be that person. By not mentioning it first, you're signaling that you're not trying to squeeze their commission — you're trying to solve their problem (a difficult property, a difficult seller, a cash buyer who doesn't need a bank and doesn't waste time).

When they ask — and good realtors will — you have the answer ready: "We work with the standard 2.5–3% agent commission. That's what you'd get on any comparable transaction." That's it. You're not cheap. You're not negotiating. You're fair and professional. But you lead the conversation with what you do (buy fast, buy as-is) not what they make. That's the difference between an operator and a hustler.

05 — Making Realtors Feel Seen

Making Realtors Feel Seen

This is the number-one skill that separates deal-getters from the pack. It's not your pitch. It's not your credentials. It's not even your offer. It's whether realtors feel understood. Realtors who feel understood and remembered bring you deals. Realtors who feel like just another contact stop returning your calls, stop thinking of you when deals come across their desk.

Strategy 1: Remember something from the last call. "Hey, how did that listing in the north end go?" or "Did you ever sell that 4-bed near downtown?" Write down details during and after calls. Three months later, reference something specific. Realtors notice when you actually listened, not just waited to pitch. It signals respect.

Strategy 2: Follow up with a non-pitch text. Three to five days after a good call, send a text: "Hey [Name], hope your week's going well" with zero ask. No deal request. No pitch. Nothing. Just a human touch. This plants a seed that you're thinking about them as a person, not just when you want something.

Strategy 3: Acknowledge the personal stuff. If they mention their dog, their family trip, a stressful listing, or anything outside of deals, reference it. "Hey, I hope you get a million bucks this weekend for you and your sellers. If not, I'll hit you up Tuesday." The specificity matters. Realtors are people, not deal machines. When you treat them like people, they remember you.

Strategy 4: Be the same person every time. If you're chatty and friendly on the first call, be that way on the fifth call. If you're professional and direct, stay consistent. Realtors notice when your personality flips based on whether you have a pending deal with them. Consistency builds trust.

The compounding effect: One realtor sending you one deal a month is a $20,000-plus annual relationship for them and exponentially more valuable to your business because they know you and trust you. You're not competing on price or conditions — you're competing on relationships. Build those relationships. Water them regularly with non-transactional touches. This is your moat.

06 — Calling Through a List (Power Dialer)

Calling Through a List (Power Dialer)

You don't just call random people or tire dial realtors you saw in a listing. You call through a smart view — a curated list in Close of realtors in your market who are most likely to have deals for you. These are labeled by market and temperature: Reno Warm, Houston Warm, Reno Cold, etc. Start with the "Warm" lists — people who have worked with you before or are warm referrals.

To start a power dial session: Open your smart view in Close. Hit the phone icon at the top right of the screen. Set your outbound number (must be set before dialing). The system shows you the next contact. Start dialing. The power dialer dials the number and connects you when they answer. You talk, log notes in Close, mark next steps, and press a button (or hang up) and you're on the next call.

Your minimum goal is 10 calls per hour when starting out. That's one call every 6 minutes on average, including hangups, voicemails, and conversations. As you get reps and your hangup-to-talk ratio improves, that number should climb to 12–15 calls per hour. Don't worry about being perfect on call one. Hit your rep count. Quality comes from repetition and feedback, not perfection.

After every call: Log it in Close immediately before the next dial. Add notes: "Has 3-bed investment property on market," "Not interested in investor deals," "Broker for off-market deals." Then go to the Call Analyzer tool and watch/listen to the recording. What did you do well? What made them sound interested? What tripped you up or made them defensive? Adjust for the next call. This feedback loop is where you improve fast.

07 — Common Objections & Responses

Common Objections & Responses

Realtors will test you. They'll throw objections to see if you're legit or just another investor wasting their time. They've heard promises before that didn't pan out. Here's what to expect and how to handle each objection professionally and efficiently.

Objection Your Response
I need you to walk the property first "I'm not a contractor. I pay someone to hang pictures at my house. My contractors charge $600–800 a day. I can't send them out until I have a contract — otherwise I'm spending a thousand bucks on inspections to find out we can't even agree on a price. That doesn't make sense at the volume I operate. Give me a 5-day DD and my contractor is there next day. We run all the inspections during that window, and if something crazy comes up, we deal with it then."
My seller wants a higher price "I totally get it. I hope they get it. Let me know if it doesn't work out — I'll be here."
I only work with owner-occupants Acknowledge the boundary. Ask permission to reach out if they get something investor-friendly. Move on. Don't waste time on this realtor.
What's your offer based on? Share your reasoning: "I'm looking at ARV in the market, the repair estimate from my guys, and comparable sales. That's where my number comes from." Never share your MAO (Maximum Allowable Offer).
I've been burned by investors before "I completely understand — and I don't want to waste your time either. Here's how we work: you bring me a deal, I run my numbers, and then I'll just ask you to verbally float the price to your seller. If they come back and say they need more, no problem — no contract gets written. But if they're close, we move forward. No wasted paperwork unless it's worth it for everyone."

Notice the pattern in all responses: Acknowledge their concern or objection (validates their experience). Answer their question directly (builds trust). Move forward or know when to exit (respects their time). You're not trying to convince anyone or overcome resistance through pressure. You're qualifying bad deals early and showing respect for their time and experience. This is why they'll call you back when something real comes up.

When "Are you still a realtor?" works and when it doesn't 3 min read
▼

Never use "Are you still a realtor?" as a cold opener. If they're in your Close smart view or you pulled them from a realtor database, they're already a realtor — asking signals you didn't do basic homework and undermines professionalism immediately. It wastes time and puts them on guard before you've even pitched.

However, you CAN use this phrase occasionally mid-call as provocation if the conversation is lagging or they seem disengaged. For example: "Look, are you still a realtor or did you switch to something else? Because if you're still in the game, I've got deals." Used this way, it's a light jab that can re-engage a dead conversation.

In SMS, it's acceptable — it's casual and can work in text outreach to cold leads. But as a first-call opener? Skip it. Assume they're active. Assume they have deals. Dive into your opening and your buy box. Move fast. This is a numbers game where professionalism and efficiency compound over time.

08 — Float a Verbal Before Writing

Float a Verbal Before Writing

This is critical and non-negotiable. Before you write a single contract, you must float the offer number verbally to the realtor. This prevents wasted paperwork, saves everyone's time, and signals to the realtor that you respect their time and their seller's time.

Floating a Verbal (Copyable)
"Based on what I'm seeing, I'm thinking somewhere around X. Does that work for your seller?"

Their answer tells you everything. If they say "Yeah, that could work" or "Yeah, let me ask them," you move forward to writing. If they say "No, they need more" and you don't want to go higher, you say "No problem, let me know if something changes" — and you mean it. Don't write a contract they'll reject. If they say "Let me ask them," that's permission to write.

The point: Never write a contract blind. Have the conversation. Get a yes, a no, or a "let me ask them." Then you decide if it's worth putting pen to paper. This saves contractors, title companies, and realtors from wasting time on offers the seller will reject.

09 — Scoring Patterns from Call Analyzer

Scoring Patterns from Call Analyzer

Good Results uses the Call Analyzer to review your calls and identify patterns that work and patterns that kill deals. Some patterns are instant reputation killers. Some patterns generate repeat calls from realtors. After every power dial session, review 2–3 calls in the analyzer and check yourself for these patterns.

Negative patterns to avoid: Using "are you still a realtor?" as a cold opener (wastes time and undermines professionalism). Using "wholesale," "wholesaler," "assign" language in cold outreach (triggers bad associations). Saying "just moved here" or "just got here" (undermines credibility). Saying "took time off" or "took a break" (creates doubt). Saying "me and my partner are trying to do..." (sounds inexperienced). Answering your own questions (kills conversation flow). Ending with only "keep me posted" (passive and vague). Note: "flip" and "fix-and-flip" ARE acceptable on calls and in follow-up SMS — just avoid them in first cold texts.

Positive patterns to build: State your name clearly in the first few seconds. Reference prior conversations with specific detail (shows you listen). Deliver the buy box on every call (consistency). Differentiate from other investors explicitly (clarity). Run comps live on the phone (credibility). Float verbal numbers before any contract (professionalism). Set specific follow-up timelines (accountability).

Each time you review a call in the analyzer, ask yourself: Which patterns showed up? Did I deliver the buy box clearly? Did the realtor sound interested or at ease? Would this realtor want to call me back? If the answer is no to any of these, that's your coaching point for the next call or next dialing session. Fix it and move on.

+
The right way to end every call 2 min read
▼

Never end a call with "keep me posted." That's passive and vague. It puts the onus entirely on them to remember you and reach out, which means they might not. Instead, end with a specific timeline: "I'm going to reach back out to you next Tuesday afternoon" or "I'll check in with you Friday morning." Specificity signals that you're organized, that you'll actually follow up, and that they can count on hearing from you.

If they have a property or a lead right now, end with: "Send that over and I'll take a look tonight. Can I get back to you by tomorrow morning?" This creates a specific next step for both of you.

If they don't have anything right now, end with: "I'm going to follow up next Tuesday. In the meantime, save my number as [Your Name], Investor — that way when something comes through, you can shoot me a text right away. Don't go searching for my number."

The key is specificity and action. They should know exactly when they're hearing from you next and why. This is how you stay top-of-mind.

10 — Scripts & Copyable Responses

Scripts & Copyable Responses

Here are all the key scripts from this module in one place, ready to copy and memorize. Use these to anchor your calls. Don't read them robotically — internalize them so they come out naturally in your own voice. The words matter less than the confidence and tone.

Walkthrough Objection
"I'm not a contractor. I pay someone to hang pictures at my house. My contractors charge $600–800 a day. I can't send them out until I have a contract — otherwise I'm spending a thousand bucks on inspections to find out we can't even agree on a price. That doesn't make sense at the volume I operate. Give me a 5-day DD and my contractor is there next day. We run all the inspections during that window, and if something crazy comes up, we deal with it then."
Burned by Investors Objection
"I completely understand — and I don't want to waste your time either. Here's how we work: you bring me a deal, I run my numbers, and then I'll just ask you to verbally float the price to your seller. If they come back and say they need more, no problem — no contract gets written. But if they're close, we move forward. No wasted paperwork unless it's worth it for everyone."
After-Sending-Offer Text
"Hey [Name], just sent over that offer on [Address]. Text me when you get the contract back and I (or my partner) will sign it same day."
Never Mention Commission Proactively

You have a standard 2.5–3% agent commission. If a realtor asks, confirm it with confidence: "We work with the standard 2.5–3% agent commission." But don't lead with it or justify it. You're not trying to negotiate them down or justify the rate. You're trying to solve their problem (difficult property, difficult seller, cash buyer). Commission is table stakes, not a conversation starter.

Keep Me Posted is NOT a Next Step

"Keep me posted" is vague and passive. It puts the burden on them to remember you and reach out. Instead, use specific timelines and actions: "I'll reach back out Tuesday afternoon" or "Send that over and I'll get back to you by tomorrow at 10am." Specificity wins deals. Vagueness loses them.
