Run a Soft Comp
Quick check on Zillow — 3 minutes max
3 min ▼

A soft comp is not a deep analysis. It's a quick 3-minute verification that the deal is in the ballpark before you move forward.

Steps to run a soft comp on Zillow:

  1. Pull up the subject property on Zillow
  2. Open the map view
  3. Draw a boundary around the immediate neighborhood (use Zillow's draw tool)
  4. Filter: Sold listings, last 12 months, similar square footage (within 10-20%)
  5. Identify 2-3 model-match sales
  6. If first comp has an ADU → discard it or adjust heavily
The Golden Rule of Soft Comps

3 minutes max. You're not building a comp analysis — you're sanity-checking the deal. Deep analysis comes later if the property moves forward.

Float the Verbal First
Test your offer number before writing contracts
2 min ▼

Never write a contract blind. Before you write anything, verbally float the offer number to the realtor to get a temperature check.

The Float Script

"Based on what I'm seeing — comps, repairs, comparable sales in the market — I'm thinking somewhere around [X]. Does that work for your seller?"

Three possible responses:

  • "Yeah, that works" — Move to Send Terms immediately
  • "Let me ask them" — Permission to write the contract
  • "No, they need more" — You now know the range; adjust if you want and float again

This saves everyone time. No rejected contracts. No wasted title work. No contractor inspections on deals that were never viable.

Confirm Buy Box & Get Clarity
Does this property fit? What questions to ask the realtor
3 min ▼

Before you send offer terms, confirm the property fits your buy box and ask the realtor clarifying questions.

Does the property fit our criteria?

  • As-is deal (no owner-occupied rehab)
  • In our approved markets (Nevada, Texas)
  • Workable margins (your MAO hits the numbers)

What to ask the realtor:

  • Any title issues, liens, or estate complications?
  • Roof/Foundation/HVAC — what's the condition?
  • Are pictures available?
  • Solar details (if applicable — payoff amount, lease vs own?)
  • Can I get into the property for a walkthrough before contract?
Get Clarity Early

These questions help you price the deal correctly and avoid surprises. A solar lease or $20k in HOA liens changes everything.

Send Offer Terms to Realtor
You send terms; they write the contract
4 min ▼

You send the realtor your offer terms (price, EMD, closing date, DD period, etc.). The realtor writes the actual contract. This is the division of labor.

What to include in your offer terms:

  • Purchase price
  • EMD (earnest money deposit) amount
  • Closing date
  • Due diligence / inspection period (5-7 days Nevada, 10 days Texas)
  • Assignment clause (keep it in — allows flexibility)
  • Investor intent clause (buyer is a real estate investor)
  • Cash offer (no financing or appraisal contingency)
  • As-is (no repair requests from seller)
Use the Right Form

Nevada: RPA (Residential Purchase Agreement)

Texas: TREC (Texas Real Estate Commission standard form)

Both forms support assignment language and investor intent clauses. Make sure your realtor uses the right state form.

Assignment Clause & Deal Structure
Why assignment matters; assignment vs. double close
3 min ▼

Keep the assignment clause in every contract. This gives you flexibility to close in different entities or transfer your contract rights to another buyer.

What is assignment? You transfer your contract rights (and any profit/fee) to another buyer/entity. Faster and cheaper than double close. Requires seller permission (covered in standard contract language).

What if the seller objects? Use this script:

The Assignment Script

"I've got multiple LLC entities and we buy a lot of homes every year. That's why I keep assignment in — I primarily close in one version of Good Results LLC but it depends on where the money is at close and who I might partner with. That's all that language is for."

What is a double close? You close on the property yourself, then immediately sell it to an end buyer the same day. You're on title briefly. No seller permission needed, but it costs more (two sets of closing costs).

When to use which: Assignment first (preferred, faster, cheaper). Double close only if assignment language is refused.

If assignment is removed from the contract but you later need to assign: escalate to Gayden. He's handled double closes close to closing.

EMD & Due Diligence Timing
When earnest money is due; protecting the inspection window
3 min ▼

Standard EMD: 1% of purchase price, typically $3,000-$5,000.

Critical Timing Rule: EMD is due at the END of the due diligence period, NOT 3 calendar days after contract. This protects you.

LocationDD WindowEMD Due
Nevada5-7 daysEnd of option period (day 5-7)
Texas10 daysEnd of due diligence (day 10)

This is not negotiable. You have days 1-7 (Nevada) or 1-10 (Texas) to inspect. Only after that inspection period ends does EMD become non-refundable.

Why This Matters

If EMD is due day 3, you're forced to wire money before you even know what the repairs cost. By making it due at the END of DD, you can walk away penalty-free if inspections reveal major issues.

If higher EMD is requested: Always ask Gayden first. It affects your deal margins.

Walkthroughs, Inspections & Escalations
How to handle realtor objections; when to escalate to Gayden
3 min ▼

Realtors sometimes ask for walkthroughs before contract or object to due diligence periods. Here's how to handle it.

If realtor asks "Can you walk the property before contract?"

The Walkthrough Script

"I'm not a contractor. I barely own a hammer. Me walking through the property doesn't tell me anything useful. My contractors charge $600-800 a day. In order to send my contractor, I need to be in contract first — otherwise I'm spending a thousand bucks on inspections to find out we can't even agree on a price. That doesn't make sense at the volume I operate. Give me a 5-day DD and my contractor is there tomorrow morning."

During due diligence, when asked about inspections:

The Inspection Script

"I just need my contractor out there — he tells me what the full remodel needs. If something crazy comes up, we deal with it then."

Keep it simple. Don't mention specialty inspections unless critical issues arise.

When to escalate to Gayden:

  • Assignment language gets removed and you now need to assign
  • Seller refuses $1 option fee and won't budge
  • Price gap too wide after inspection; you wanted X, comps support X, seller won't move
  • Title issues come up (liens, estates, probate, HOA problems)

Send Contract & Protect Your MAO
Where to route contracts; why you never share your ceiling price
2 min ▼

After the realtor writes the contract and you've reviewed it:

Text the realtor: "Hey [Name], just sent over that offer on [Address]. Text me when you get the contract back and I (or my partner) will sign it same day."

Send the signed contract to: contracts@goodresults.org

This ensures both Gayden and Joe get it. Single-recipient errors lose deals.

Never share your Maximum Allowable Offer (MAO). MAO is your absolute highest price given your repair estimates and target profit. It's internal.

What to share instead: your reasoning.

The Transparency Script

"I'm looking at ARV in the market, the repair estimate from my contractors, and comparable sales data. That's how I came to this number."

Why this matters: If you share your MAO ($250k) and the realtor knows you'll go higher, they'll shop your offer to get sellers to reject it so they can bring you back for a higher counter. Keeping your ceiling secret prevents this negotiating game.
