01 — The Five-Step Offer Process

The Five-Step Offer Process

Once a realtor has brought you a property and you've decided to pursue it, you follow a systematic five-step process to get into contract. Each step has a purpose. Skip a step and deals fall apart. Cut corners and you lose money at closing. Follow the process and you control the deal lifecycle.

The five steps are: (1) Run a soft comp to verify the deal makes sense. (2) Float the verbal offer to get the realtor's (and seller's) initial reaction. (3) Confirm the property fits your buy box criteria. (4) Write the contract and send for signature. (5) Send a follow-up text to confirm execution and set expectations for next steps.

Timing matters. Speed matters. But accuracy matters more. You'd rather lose a deal because you took an extra 15 minutes to verify numbers than close a terrible deal because you rushed the comp. This module walks you through each step with precision.

02 — Step 1: Run a Soft Comp (3 minutes max)

Step 1: Run a Soft Comp (3 minutes max)

A soft comp is a quick check to know if a deal is worth pursuing. Should take no more than 3 minutes. Not a deep analysis — just a quick verification.

How to run a soft comp on Zillow: Pull up the subject property on Zillow. Switch to map view. Use Zillow's draw tool to draw a boundary around the immediate neighborhood (typically a 5-10 minute radius depending on the neighborhood). Filter the view: Sold listings only, last 12 months sold, similar square footage (within 10–20% of the subject). Identify 2–3 model-match sales in that area. These become your comparables. Do quick math: If comparables are selling in the $500–550k range and you're offering $380k on a property that needs $80k in repairs, that's $460k all-in, which makes sense. Or if comps are $350k and you're offering $300k with $100k repairs, that's $400k all-in, which is too high. The soft comp tells you if you're in the ballpark.

Critical detail: If the first comp has an ADU (accessory dwelling unit), discard it or adjust heavily. ADUs add value that a standard property doesn't have. Use properties without major additions as your comparables.

03 — Step 2: Float the Verbal

Step 2: Float the Verbal

Before writing anything, call the realtor and float your offer number verbally. This is the same verbal float you learned in Module 2, but now you're actually executing it after running numbers.

Float Verbal Script (Copyable)
"Based on what I'm seeing with comps and repairs, I'm thinking somewhere around $[NUMBER]. Does that work for your seller?"

Their answer tells you everything. If they say "Yeah, that could work" or "Yeah, let me ask them," you have permission to write. If they say "No, they need more" and you don't want to go higher, you say "No problem, let me know if something changes." If they say "He actually wants X," and X is higher than you want to go, you have a negotiation moment. You can counter: "Let me run those numbers, but I'm thinking closer to Y." The verbal float prevents wasted paperwork.

04 — Step 3: Confirm Buy Box Fit

Step 3: Confirm Buy Box Fit

Before you write the contract, confirm the property fits all your buy box criteria. Does it meet your square footage requirements? Is it in one of your markets? Are the numbers workable? Are there any title issues, liens, or estate complications the realtor knows about? Get clarity on everything that could kill the deal at closing.

Questions to ask the realtor: "Any title issues I should know about?" "Estate situation or standard sale?" "Solar equipment on the roof?" "Any HOA?" "How is the condition on the roof, foundation, and HVAC?" You're not ordering an inspection yet — you're qualifying the property and the deal structure.

This is also where you confirm the realtor can deliver photos (from listing or realtor walkthrough). You'll need those for your team and for contractors during due diligence. If the listing doesn't have quality photos and the realtor can't provide them, that's a sign this might not be a serious deal yet.

05 — Step 4: Write the Contract

Step 4: Write the Contract

Now that you've floated the verbal and confirmed buy box fit, you write the contract. The form matters based on state. Always include an assignment clause (allows you to close in different entities or assign rights if needed). Always include an investor intent clause (states that the buyer is a real estate investor, does not intend to occupy the property as primary residence).

Nevada: Use the RPA (Residential Purchase Agreement) form. This is the standard Nevada real estate form. Texas: Use the TREC (Texas Real Estate Commission) standard form. Both allow for assignment clauses and investor intent language. Keep both clauses in your standard contract. If a seller or realtor objects to the assignment language, you can offer to remove it — but then you'll need to use a double-close structure at closing to protect yourself from liability.

Critical clauses: Cash offer (no financing contingency). As-is condition (no repair requests from buyer). No appraisal contingency. Due diligence window (typically 5–7 days in Nevada, 10 days in Texas, but negotiable). EMD (earnest money deposit) amount and deadline.

Send the contract to contracts@goodresults.org so both Gayden and Joe receive it. Never send contracts to just one person — this prevents lost deals.

After Sending Offer (Copyable)

06 — Earnest Money Deposit (EMD) — Standard & Timing

Earnest Money Deposit (EMD) — Standard & Timing

EMD is money deposited with the escrow company to show you're serious about the purchase. It's non-refundable after the due diligence period ends. Standard EMD is 1% of purchase price, typically $3,000–$5,000. If a deal needs a higher EMD, always ask Gayden first before agreeing.

Critical timing: EMD is due at the END of the option period (Nevada) or due diligence period (Texas). NOT 3 calendar days from contract. NOT before you've had time to inspect. The EMD deadline is written in the contract — it's typically 7–10 days out depending on your DD window. You have time to run inspections before forfeiting that money. This is non-negotiable in every contract you write.

EMD Timing is Critical — Not 3 Calendar Days

Never let a title company or realtor push EMD to 3 calendar days from contract. EMD is due at the END of your inspection/option period. That means: day 1 through day 7 (or 10) you inspect. On day 8 (or 11), if you haven't exercised your right to terminate, EMD becomes non-refundable. This is your contingency period. Protect it.

+
What happens if you need to request more EMD? 2 min read
▼

Sometimes a seller will request higher EMD (2–3% instead of 1%) to show you're serious. Or a Title company may default to higher EMD based on their company policy. If EMD needs to go higher than 1%, you stop and ask Gayden before committing. He may approve it for a strong deal. He may have you negotiate it down. Never commit to higher EMD without approval — it's corporate money, not your personal money, and it affects deal margins.

07 — The $1 Option Fee Objection

The $1 Option Fee Objection

Some realtors or sellers will push back on language that says "option fee" is just $1. They see it as cheap or disrespectful. You need to be ready to explain it confidently.

$1 Option Fee Response (Copyable)
"I've got multiple LLC entities and we buy a lot of homes every year. That's why I keep assignment in — I primarily close in one version of Good Results LLC but it depends on where the money is at close and who I might partner with. That's all that language is for."

If a seller absolutely won't accept $1 and won't budge on assignment language, this is when you escalate to Gayden. He has successfully negotiated these situations and may have you add $500–$1,000 to the offer price instead of removing the clause. The goal: keep assignment language intact because it gives you flexibility at closing.

08 — The Walkthrough Objection (Full Script)

The Walkthrough Objection (Full Script)

Realtors sometimes say the seller wants you (or their own contractor) to walk the property before signing. Use this full script to push back professionally and explain why that doesn't make sense in your offer structure.

Walkthrough Objection (Copyable)
"I'm not a contractor. I barely own a hammer. Me walking through the property doesn't tell me anything useful. My contractors charge $600–800 a day. In order to send my contractor, I need to be in contract first — otherwise I'm spending a thousand bucks on inspections to find out we can't even agree on a price. That doesn't make sense at the volume I operate. Give me a 5–7 day DD and my contractor is there tomorrow morning."

The logic is sound: You're not qualified to assess repair scope. Your contractors are. But you can't send them until you're in contract because the cost is too high otherwise. This is a fundamental principle of your business model. Realtors understand it once they hear it. If they still push, they might not be a good realtor partner for your business model.

09 — Assignment vs. Double Close

Assignment vs. Double Close

These are your two mechanisms for closing a property. Understanding when to use each is critical.

Aspect Assignment Double Close
Definition You transfer your contract rights to another buyer/entity. You close on property yourself, then sell same-day to another buyer.
Seller Permission Required (in contract language) Not required
Cost Assignment fee to buyer (typically $5-$20k) Two full closing costs (double costs, not typical)
Timeline Faster — one contract to two closings Slower — requires parallel title/escrow setup
Seller Visibility Seller aware you're assigning (per contract) Seller unaware of double close (you're the buyer on record)
Good Results Standard Preferred method — always include in contracts Backup only — use if assignment refused

Assignment is your primary strategy. You write the contract with assignment language (you can assign to another buyer/entity). When the end buyer comes through, you assign your rights to them for a fee. This is faster and cheaper than a double close. If a seller refuses assignment language, you can offer a double close — you buy it, they get their money same day, and your end buyer closes with you. It's more expensive but sometimes necessary.

(2) The $1 option fee is refused and the seller won't budge. Gayden may structure the deal differently. (3) Price gap is too wide after inspection. You wanted $X, comps support $X, but seller won't move. Gayden decides if you walk or restructure. (4) Title issues come up that aren't obvious. Liens, estates, probate, HOA problems. Gayden has connections and may solve these. Don't try to navigate these alone — Gayden has done it a hundred times.

10 — Never Share Your MAO

Never Share Your MAO

MAO is your Maximum Allowable Offer — the absolute highest price you'll pay on a deal given your repair estimates and target profit. It's internal. Never share it with a realtor, seller, or anyone outside your team.

What to share instead: Your reasoning. "I'm looking at ARV in the market, the repair estimate from my contractors, and comparable sales data. That's how I came to this number." This is transparent and professional. You're not hiding your logic — you're just not revealing your profit target.

Why this matters: If you share your MAO ($250k) and the realtor knows you'll go higher, they'll shop your offer around trying to get sellers to reject it so they can bring you back with a higher counter. Keeping your ceiling secret prevents this game.

MAO is Internal — Share Reasoning Instead

You can explain how you arrived at your number without revealing your true ceiling. "I looked at comparable sales in the neighborhood ($450–500k), repair costs ($80–100k based on age/condition), and my holding/closing costs. That's how I got to this offer." This satisfies the realtor's need to understand your methodology without giving away your negotiating room.
