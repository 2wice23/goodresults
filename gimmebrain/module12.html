Distressed Situation Types

Recognize what you're dealing with
4 min
↓

Every distressed deal falls into one of these buckets. Your job is to identify which type it is, because that tells you the seller's pain point and how to pitch the solution.

Situation Motivation Realtor's Tell
Foreclosure / Pre-Foreclosure Very High "Needs to close before bank takes it"
Probate / Estate Sale High "Executors just want it gone"
Divorce Liquidation High "Both parties want it done"
Tax Foreclosure Pending Very High "Unpaid property taxes"
Underwater Mortgage High "They owe more than it's worth"
Inherited / Absentee Moderate "Owner doesn't live here"
High DOM (4+ months) Moderate-High "No offers in months"

The higher the motivation, the more likely they'll accept your offer fast and the less likely they'll haggle.

A. Speed and certainty before the bank takes over

The Distressed Deal Pitch

How to position your offer
3 min
↓

In distressed situations, the seller's pain point is certainty and speed. Position around those two things.

"Cash buyer. As-is. 10–21 day close. We handle the complexity — title issues, liens, estate paperwork, whatever it is. We close. That's what we do."

This pitch works because it:

  • Cash = No financing delays
  • As-is = No inspection contingencies holding things up
  • 10–21 days = Specific, believable timeline
  • "We handle the complexity" = You're the problem-solver, not another headache

Handling Liens & Assignment Language

Don't let contract language kill the deal
3 min
↓

Some sellers (especially in estate/probate situations) will object to assignment language in the contract. They may also object to language suggesting the buyer is an investor who might resell quickly.

Gayden's take: "I keep that language in not because I'm trying to do something shady — I might close in any of six different LLC entities I own. If the word 'assignment' is the issue, I'll happily remove it. But I need the rest of the language because I'm not presenting myself as an owner-occupant. I'm an investor. I'm going to make money. You need to know that."

The key insight: Be transparent about your role. Don't hide that you're an investor, but do remove language that makes them uncomfortable if it doesn't affect the deal's core structure.

Red Flags & Motivation Signals

Read between the lines
2 min
↓

Learn to read between the lines when realtors describe a situation:

  • Delinquent taxes or HOA fees → Financial stress. They need a fast close.
  • "As-is listing" → Seller can't or won't do repairs. No negotiating timeline.
  • Property sits vacant → Holding costs with no income. Pain is real.
  • Long DOM at reduced price → Retail market rejected it. Room for your offer.
  • Estate, divorce, or probate → Emotional and legal pressure to close.
  • Tenant damage with deferred maintenance → Overwhelmed landlord. Good candidate.

Manufactured Homes & Know Your Limits

When to escalate to Gayden
2 min
↓

Manufactured homes (mobile homes, modulars) are a different animal. Financing, title, liens, and repairs are all different. If you're not experienced with them, escalate to Gayden.

Rule of thumb: If the property is on a foundation (stick-built house) or has a traditional mortgage with a standard title, you can handle it. If it's a manufactured home with any complexity, ask Gayden first.

Why it matters: Manufactured home loans are harder to finance, liens are harder to clear, and repairs are subject to different codes. It's not worth the headache if you're not an expert.

Complex Estate & Probate Deals

The executor wants it gone
3 min

Estate and probate deals are some of the best distressed opportunities because executors have legal and financial incentives to close fast. But they also come with complexity.

What makes them different:

  • Multiple heirs may need to agree (leverage for speed)
  • Court approval may be required (timing is tight)
  • Executor liability is real (they want the property off their hands)
  • Property condition is often poor (years of deferred maintenance)

Your positioning: "We specialize in estate properties. Cash, as-is, fast close. You're off the hook."

Executors understand liability and want certainty. That's your advantage.

Timeline Mastery & Communication

Close the deal in writing
3 min
↓

Once you've got a seller interested, your pitch becomes about the timeline. Here's how to communicate it:

Day 1: We send the contract (2-hour turnaround after your realtor's approval) Day 2-3: You sign, we verify title and liens (instant if clean) Day 4-7: Appraisal and inspection (if needed for financing) Day 8-14: Underwriting and final walkthrough Day 15-21: Closing (we handle all escrow and document signing)

What this messaging does:

  • Shows you're fast but not reckless
  • Sets expectations (no surprises)
  • Removes objections before they come up

Build Long-Term Realtor Relationships

They bring you the deals
2 min
↓

Realtors are your best source for distressed deals. They know the market, they know the motivated sellers, and they know when someone is desperate.

How to work with realtors:

  • Respond fast (same day or next morning)
  • Close what you say you'll close
  • Pay commission on time, no excuses
  • Refer them other agents when you know people
  • Make it easy for them (clear contract, no drama)

The payoff: Good realtors will call you first for their distressed listings. That's where the best deals live.
