Section 1

Distressed Situation Types

Distressed deals are your bread and butter. They're deals where realtors have been stuck, sellers need help, and you can build lifetime relationships. Learn to spot the situations, understand the motivation levels, and respond with confidence.

Situation Motivation Red Flags & Signals
Foreclosure / Pre-Foreclosure Very High Bank filing notice, delinquent payments, seller needs to close before auction date
Probate / Estate Sale High Executors/heirs just want it liquidated, long court process, as-is condition
Divorce Liquidation High Both parties need clean separation, settlement deadline, below-market listing
Tax Foreclosure Pending Very High Unpaid property taxes, tax sale approaching, countdown clock visible
Underwater Mortgage High Seller owes more than it's worth, short sale scenario, relocation urgency
Inherited / Absentee Moderate Owner doesn't live here, inherited recently, out-of-state seller
Distressed Listing (High DOM) Moderate-High On market 90+ days, price reduced multiple times, property sits vacant

The more motivation signals you see, the more powerful your positioning. A realtor with a nightmare estate deal sees you close cleanly, and you've got a partner for life.

Section 2

The Distressed Deal Pitch

Your positioning is simple and powerful: "Cash buyer. As-is. 10–21 day close. We handle the complexity — title issues, liens, estate paperwork, whatever it is. We close. That's what we do."

In distressed situations, the seller's pain point is certainty and speed. Position around those two things every single time. Don't negotiate on these pillars.

Quick-Response Playbook
Estate sale: "That's a great fit for us. Cash, as-is, 10–21 days. Executors love that." Tax foreclosure: "We can move fast. What's the timeline before tax sale?" Divorce: "Quick close, no repairs required, cash. That's exactly what we do." Judgment lien: "What's the lien amount? That comes out at closing — not a deal-killer." Long DOM: "What are they willing to take? Let's run the numbers."
Section 3

The Assignment Clause & Investor Language Issue

Some sellers (especially estate/probate) object to assignment language. They worry you're going to flip the contract to someone else. Here's Gayden's explanation:

Script — Addressing Assignment Concerns
"I keep that language in not because I'm trying to do something shady — I might close in any of six different LLC entities I own. If the word 'assignment' is the issue, I'll happily remove it. But I need the rest of the language because I'm not presenting myself as an owner-occupant. I'm an investor. I'm going to make money. You need to know that."

Honesty + confidence disarms objections. Most sellers respect the directness.

Section 5

Building Long-Term Realtor Relationships From Distressed Deals

Distressed deals are where trust is built fastest. A realtor with a nightmare estate deal — liens, deferred maintenance, executor drama, title mess — who sees you close cleanly and on time will never forget you. That's the deal that turns a cold contact into a loyal source.

Your advantage: most investors avoid these deals because they're complex. You show up, you handle the chaos, you close on time. Realtors remember that.

1

Communicate every step. Don't ghost. Daily updates if needed.

2

Close on time. Period. No excuses. Complex doesn't mean late.

3

Make the realtor look like a hero to their client. "Thanks for getting us the right buyer" vibe.

+
What actually happens in a foreclosure situation?
Pre-foreclosure timeline and your role
▼

Pre-foreclosure: the bank has filed a notice of default/foreclosure. The seller has weeks or months before the foreclosure auction. If they don't close before that date, they lose the property and all equity.

Time is literally money. Seller owes $200K on a $250K house, they have 60 days before auction. You offer $240K cash, close in 20 days. Seller keeps $40K. Without you, they get $0.

Your positioning: "We understand the timeline. We can move fast. What's the date we're working against?" Confidence about speed wins these deals.

+
Probate and estate deals — the hidden complexity
Why court approval matters

Probate is the legal process of settling a deceased person's estate. The executor (or administrator if no will) must get court approval to sell. This can take weeks or months depending on the state and court backlog.

Realtors working probate deals already know this complexity. Position as a patient, reliable buyer who understands the process. Your speed is still valuable — you don't jerk around with inspections or second-guess financing.

Executors are often family members dealing with grief + legal bureaucracy. Your professionalism and follow-through become even more valuable.
