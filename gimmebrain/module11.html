Section 1

What Due Diligence Is and Why It Matters

Due Diligence (DD) is the inspection and verification period between contract and closing. Its purpose: verify the property matches your investment thesis — NOT to manufacture price reductions. Never use DD as a routine price-chipping tool. Realtors talk. A reputation for always coming back with a "surprise" makes you radioactive.

DD is your chance to validate the deal. If the numbers don't work or you discover something material, you have the contractual right to walk away cleanly. Use it that way — not as a negotiation tactic.

Critical Rule

DD is NOT a negotiation tactic. Realtors have been burned by buyers who use DD to chip price. Don't be that buyer. Good Results' competitive advantage is being the buyer who does what they say.

Section 2

The DD Timeline (7-Day Example)

Day Activity
Day 1 Contract accepted. EMD sent to escrow. Title search ordered. General inspection scheduled.
Days 1–3 General home inspection (structure, roof, HVAC, electrical, plumbing)
Days 2–5 Specialty inspections if needed (roof engineer, HVAC specialist, structural, mold, environmental)
Days 3–7 Title commitment received. Payoff letters requested. Contractor repair estimates gathered.
Day 7–8 Internal decision point: proceed, request credit, or terminate. Realtor gets clear answer.
After DD Move to closing. Earnest money becomes non-refundable (in Nevada/Texas terms).

This timeline works for most deals. Complex deals (major repairs, title issues, environmental concerns) may need extension — ask Gayden.

Section 3

Repair Cost Framework & Decision Matrix

Use this framework to decide: proceed, request credit, or walk.

Repair Cost Action
< $5,000 Proceed Not worth jeopardizing relationship
$5,000–$20,000 Discuss Talk with Gayden. May request credit or proceed.
> $20,000 or structural Escalate Call Gayden immediately. Walk or renegotiate.

Specific findings guide: Cosmetic (paint, flooring) = proceed. HVAC replacement = already estimated, proceed. Roof work = already estimated, proceed. Foundation issues = call Gayden immediately. Mold, termites, structural damage = call Gayden. Unpermitted additions = escalate to Gayden (title/escrow issue).

Script — Setting Expectations at Contract
"I'll have an answer for you by [Day 7]. If I find anything significant, I'll let you know immediately. I won't leave you hanging."
Section 4

Communicating With the Realtor

The realtor is your communication window. Set expectations at contract signing. Then maintain momentum.

1

If Terminating: "The inspection findings don't align with our investment criteria. We're walking away cleanly."

2

If Proceeding: "Inspection came back fine. We're moving forward as planned. What's the update on [next closing step]?"

3

If Still Deciding: "Hey, still working through the inspection findings — will have a clear answer for you by tomorrow morning."

Golden Rule

NEVER GHOST. Not for a day, not for half a day. A realtor who doesn't hear from you for 48 hours assumes the deal is dying. Keep them updated.

Section 5

DD in Nevada vs. Texas

Different states, different rules. Know which state you're operating in.

Nevada (RPA)

Due Diligence Period: Free cancellation right during DD period. $0 option fee. Just give notice before deadline.

Texas (TREC)

Option Period: Costs $1.00 but buyer has unrestricted right to terminate. Option fee + EMD both due at end of option/DD period (NOT 3 calendar days from acceptance).

Critical Texas note: Missing the option/DD period deadline for option fee + EMD means the seller can terminate contract. Don't let your escrow officer miss this.

Why do realtors hate buyers who use DD to chip price?
The psychology and reputation damage
▼

Every realtor has a client who accepted a lower offer, then got nickel-and-dimed during DD. "Oh, inspector found issues, we need $15K reduction." It's a bait-and-switch that destroys trust.

The moment a realtor sees a pattern of you using DD as a negotiation tactic, they stop sending you deals. You become "that buyer." And in a relationship business, reputation is everything.

Good Results' competitive advantage is being the buyer who does what they say. Your offer is your word. Stand behind it.

+
Foundation issues — what they mean and what to do
Why you can't assess alone

Foundation problems range from minor settling (hairline cracks that are cosmetic) to structural failure (house is sinking, water is getting in). You can't assess them without a structural engineer.

Cost range: minor crack fill = $3K. Pier and beam or full underpinning = $50K+. That's a massive spread. Don't make a unilateral decision. Call Gayden immediately.

Gayden has relationships with foundation contractors and knows which issues are deal-killers vs. solvable. You don't. Let him decide.
