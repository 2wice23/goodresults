The Purpose of DD
What due diligence really means in a deal
2 min▼

Due Diligence (DD) is the inspection and verification period between contract and closing. The purpose is to verify the property matches your investment thesis — not to manufacture price reductions.

Core Principle

DD is your safety valve. If the property doesn't match what you thought you were buying, you have a limited window to walk away before earnest money becomes non-refundable. Use it for verification, not haggling.

Your contractors will tell you what they find. Your job is to decide: proceed as-is, request a credit, ask to renegotiate, or terminate. And communicate your decision clearly to the realtor — no ghosting.

DD Timeline (7-Day Example)
What happens when and who's responsible
3 min▼

A typical DD period lasts 7 days from contract acceptance. Here's the sequence:

TimelineActivity
Day 1Contract accepted. EMD sent to escrow. Title search ordered. General inspection scheduled.
Days 1–3General home inspection
Days 2–5Specialty inspections if needed (roof, HVAC, structural, environmental)
Days 3–7Title commitment received. Payoff letters requested. Contractor repair estimates gathered.
Day 7–8Internal decision point: proceed, request credit, or terminate. Realtor gets clear answer.
After DDMove to closing. Earnest money becomes non-refundable.

The most critical moment is Day 7–8 when you decide. Once you pass this window and move to closing, the deal is locked in. Your realtor needs a clear answer, not a stall.

What Contractors Are Looking For
The six categories inspectors always evaluate
3 min▼

Your contractor will evaluate every property against the same checklist. These are the six critical areas:

1. Roof — Age, damage, evidence of leaks. A roof older than 20 years typically needs replacement ($15,000–$40,000).

2. HVAC — Age and condition. If it's 30+ years old, budget replacement. A 1992 HVAC on a deal means factor in $15,000–$25,000.

3. Foundation — Any cracks, settling, or moisture intrusion. This is the big red flag. Cracks mean structural issues.

4. Plumbing — Leaks, pipe age, water pressure, code compliance. Galvanized pipes from the 1970s may need replacement.

5. Electrical — Panel age, breaker condition, code compliance. Outdated panels (especially Federal Pacific) are safety hazards.

6. Structural Integrity — Sagging beams, rot, termite damage, wall shifts. Any deviation from plumb or square is a concern.

Pro Tip

Get estimates for major repairs before you decide. Knowing the actual cost is critical — don't just guess or rely on rough contractor quotes.

Repair Cost Framework
How to respond when surprises exceed estimates
3 min▼

During DD, if the contractor finds surprises beyond what was estimated at offer, use this framework:

Minor surprises (under $5,000 off estimate): Proceed as-is. Not worth jeopardizing the relationship or your deal momentum. You built a margin for this.

Moderate surprises ($5,000–$20,000 off): Discuss with Gayden. You may request a credit from the seller or ask to renegotiate the price. Be strategic — the seller may walk too.

Major surprises (over $20,000 off, or structural/foundation issues): Gayden decides. Could walk, could renegotiate, could proceed depending on your other options and the deal margin.

Warning

Don't use DD as a routine price-chipping tool. Realtors talk. A reputation for always coming back with a "surprise" makes you radioactive. You'll get fewer and worse deals.

Walk vs. Negotiate vs. Proceed
Decision matrix for common inspection findings
4 min▼

When your contractor reports findings, here's how to respond to each type:

FindingRecommended Action
Cosmetic only (paint, flooring, landscaping)Proceed
HVAC needs replacementAlready factored in $35–60K — proceed or get Gayden's approval to ask for credit
Roof needs workAlready factored in — proceed
Foundation issuesCall Gayden immediately. Do not decide alone.
Significant hidden damage (mold, termites, structural)Call Gayden immediately. Likely walk.
Unpermitted additionsTitle/escrow issue, not just inspection. Call Gayden.

The key rule: cosmetic stuff and expected repairs are fine. Foundation, mold, termites, and permit issues require leadership call. Don't guess on the big ones.

Communicating With the Realtor
Scripts for every DD scenario
3 min▼

Communication is everything during DD. Here's how to handle each scenario:

Set expectations at contract signing: "I'll have an answer for you by [Day 7]. If I find anything significant, I'll let you know immediately. I won't leave you hanging."

If you're proceeding: "Inspection came back fine. We're moving forward as planned. What's the update on [next closing step]?"

If you're requesting a credit: "We found some unexpected repairs. Can we get a $[X] credit, or do we need to renegotiate? Let me know what the seller thinks."

If you're terminating: "The inspection findings don't align with our investment criteria. We're walking away cleanly."

If you're still deciding: "Hey, still working through the inspection findings — will have a clear answer for you by tomorrow morning."

Non-Negotiable Rule

Never ghost a realtor. Not for a day, not for half a day. Even if you're still deciding, communicate. This relationship is everything in this business.

Red Flags vs. Normal Wear
What stops a deal vs. what you can live with
3 min▼

Not every finding is a deal-killer. Learn to distinguish:

Deal-Killer Red Flags: Foundation cracks, significant water intrusion, mold, termite/pest infestation, unpermitted structural work, HVAC/roof beyond repair, electrical hazards, radon or asbestos.

Normal Wear (Proceed & Budget): Missing caulk, cracked grout, worn flooring, dated fixtures, HVAC at end of life but functioning, roof 15–20 years, cosmetic damage, outdated wiring.

Your Margin: You built a 20–30% margin into your offer to cover repairs. Use it. If the numbers still work, proceed. If they don't, now's the time to walk or renegotiate.

Key Principle

DD is not about finding a perfect house. It's about confirming the numbers still work and there are no hidden threats. If both are true, you're good.

After DD — What's Next
Moving from inspection to closing
2 min▼

Once you pass the DD window and give the realtor your decision, the sequence is clear:

If you proceed: You're moving to closing. Earnest money is now non-refundable. Coordinate with your lender, title company, and inspector. Confirm all closing details 48 hours before.

If you request a credit and it's accepted: The credit reduces your purchase price at closing. Document the amount agreed to in writing — don't leave it verbal.

If you renegotiate the price: This goes back through the offer process. Be clear and offer quickly so the seller doesn't shop your offer to another buyer.

If you walk: You walk cleanly and on time. Your earnest money is returned. The realtor moves on. It happens. Don't burn the bridge by disappearing or being unclear.

After you decide, your job is to execute. Get the appraisal done, finalize financing, and show up at closing. DD is over. The deal is locked in.
